Corsican Pine

Pinus nigra subsp. laricio Maire

Quick Facts

Native Range: Sicily, Corsica, Southern Italy, Southern Europe
Introduced to Britain: 1759
Uses: Forestry, Parkland cultivation
Conservation Status: Declining due to Red Band Needle Blight

About This Tree

Introduced to the British Isles in 1759, the Corsican Pine was cultivated for forestry and parkland use. This magnificent conifer has played an important role in British forestry for over two centuries.

A commercially important tree, the Corsican Pine faces challenges from a disease called 'Red Band Needle Blight' which has led to a decline in planting. Despite these challenges, Corsican Pine plantations support a high number of invertebrate species, making them ecologically valuable.

The Bordered White Moth (Bupalus piniaria L.) is particularly associated with this species, demonstrating the important ecological relationships that develop between trees and their associated wildlife.

Conservation Note

Pinus nigra is considered invasive in New Zealand, where it shades out native grasses and plants, highlighting the importance of understanding ecological context when introducing species to new environments.

In Our Gardens

At the Glasgow Botanic Gardens, we maintain a wide range of pine species as part of our comprehensive tree collection. Our arboretum features notable specimens including:

  • Pinus wallichiana (Bhutan Pine) - grows naturally in the Himalayas and is occasionally used to produce turpentine
  • Pinus coulteri - a distinctive species from California
  • Pinus ponderosa (Western Yellow Pine) - originally collected by David Douglas in 1826 from eastern Washington

This diverse collection allows visitors to compare different pine species and understand their varied adaptations and uses.
