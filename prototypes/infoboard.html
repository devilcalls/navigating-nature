Accessible Information Board

Integrated Lectern Design for Glasgow Botanic Gardens

Project Overview: The Accessible Information Board is a multi-sensory information point designed to replace traditional visual-only signs at Glasgow Botanic Gardens. These lecterns provide rich, accessible information about plants and garden history for all visitors.

Our Accessible Solution

Recognizing these critical gaps, our team has designed a comprehensive accessible information board system that addresses each of these limitations while maintaining the aesthetic beauty and educational mission of Glasgow Botanic Gardens.

Our Accessible Solution: Multi-sensory information board designed for universal access

Design Purpose

Positioned at key exhibits throughout Glasgow Botanic Gardens, these lecterns serve as primary information sources that bridge physical and digital content. Each board tells the story of specific plants while connecting visitors to the garden's rich 200-year history.

Glasgow Botanic Gardens Heritage

Founded in 1817 by Thomas Hopkirk, a distinguished Glasgow botanist, the gardens began on an 8-acre site at Sandyford with support from local dignitaries and the University of Glasgow. Hopkirk donated over 3,000 plants, which formed the core of the garden's first collections. His vision was to create a garden for the city that would serve as a site for education to enrich the university's botany and medical teaching.

The gardens moved to their present site in 1842, where they have continued to grow and serve the community for over 180 years, making them one of Scotland's most treasured botanical collections.

Key Features

  • Ergonomic Design: Angled at comfortable height for all visitors including wheelchair users
  • Weather-Resistant Materials: Matte-finish bronze or etched zinc reduces glare
  • Dual-Format Text: Large print with complete Grade 2 Braille transcription
  • Tactile QR Codes: Raised borders help blind visitors locate digital content
  • Digital Integration: Links to audio descriptions and sign language videos

Implementation at Glasgow Botanic Gardens

Each board will be positioned at significant plants and locations throughout the 27-acre site, providing visitors with comprehensive information about:

  • Individual plant species and their origins
  • Historical significance within the garden's collection
  • Connection to the garden's educational mission since 1817
  • Seasonal changes and botanical characteristics
  • Links to the garden's research and conservation work

User Experience Example

Visiting the Kibble Palace Area

Scenario: A visitor approaches one of the historic trees near the famous Kibble Palace glasshouse.

Visual reader: Reads about the tree's species, when it was planted, and its connection to the garden's Victorian-era expansion.

Braille reader: Accesses the same detailed information through tactile reading.

Digital user: Scans the QR code to hear an audio description of the tree's scale and seasonal changes, or watch a sign language interpretation of its historical significance to Glasgow's botanical heritage.

Benefits

The Accessible Information Board ensures that all visitors to Glasgow Botanic Gardens, regardless of their abilities, can fully appreciate both the natural beauty and rich history of this treasured Glasgow institution. By combining traditional materials with modern technology, these boards honor the garden's heritage while embracing inclusive design principles.

This approach transforms how visitors experience the gardens, creating opportunities for deeper engagement with both the plants and the stories they represent in Glasgow's cultural and educational landscape.
